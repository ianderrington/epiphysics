# The Hidden Energy of Knowing: Why Information Has Weight

*A conversation with my son about scattered crayons led to a profound question: What does it cost to know things?*

## The Crayon Problem

Picture this: My son Lincoln has been coloring, and now there are crayons everywhere. Some rolled under the couch. Others are behind the toy box. A few made it to the kitchen somehow. 

Now it's cleanup time.

If I **don't know** where the crayons are, I have to search the whole house. I walk back and forth, check under furniture, peek behind doors. I might cover 200 feet of walking and burn 15-20 calories doing it.

But if I **do know** where every crayon is—if I watched them roll away or remember seeing them—I can walk directly to each one. Maybe I only walk 50 feet and burn 5 calories.

That difference—those 10-15 saved calories—is the **energy value of my knowledge**.

## Information Isn't Free

Here's where it gets interesting: That knowledge isn't magic. It exists physically in my brain as specific patterns of neural connections. And those patterns have weight.

Not metaphorical weight. Actual, physical, thermodynamic weight.

But acquiring and using that knowledge costs far more than just storing it. When I see the crayons scatter and remember where they went, my brain:
- Processes light through millions of photoreceptors (~0.001 joules per crayon)
- Fires millions of neurons to recognize "that's a crayon" (~0.001 joules)
- Encodes the memory of locations (~0.0000001 joules)

**Total cost to acquire knowledge of 20 crayon locations: about 0.02 joules** (20 millijoules).

**Value of that knowledge: saves me about 28,000 joules** (28 kilojoules) of walking energy.

That's a **1.4 million-fold return on investment**. I spend 0.02 joules of brain energy to save 28,000 joules of muscle energy. This is why brains evolved—even crude information processing provides massive energy advantages.

## Maxwell's Demon and the Cost of Forgetting

In the 1800s, physicist James Clerk Maxwell imagined a tiny demon who could watch molecules bouncing around in a box. The demon could open and close a door, letting fast molecules through one way and slow molecules the other way. Without doing any work, just by **knowing** which molecules were which, the demon could make one side hot and the other cold—apparently violating the laws of thermodynamics.

Scientists puzzled over this for over a century. The solution turned out to be profound: The demon doesn't violate anything, because **the demon's memory eventually fills up**. To keep working, it has to erase old memories to make room for new observations. 

And **erasing information requires energy**.

This is Landauer's Principle, named after physicist Rolf Landauer: You can't destroy information without paying an energy cost. The demon has to pay the thermodynamic price eventually.

## Why This Matters

This isn't just abstract physics. It connects to real phenomena:

**Why computation generates heat**: Every time a computer processes information, it generates heat. While the fundamental Landauer limit is tiny, real processors operate billions of times per second, far from equilibrium. The heat comes from transistor switching, wire resistance, and operating at high speeds—but the Landauer principle sets the absolute floor on how efficient computation can ever be.

**Why thinking burns calories**: Your brain is an information-processing machine. The 20 watts your brain uses (about 20% of your body's energy) isn't just for keeping neurons alive—much of it pays for moving ions, firing neurons, and maintaining synapses. Information processing requires physical work.

**Why life is expensive**: Living things maintain their organization by constantly gathering information about their environment and responding to it. A bacterium swimming toward sugar is using information to fight entropy. That costs energy. Every organism must pay the thermodynamic price for acquiring and using knowledge.

**Why AI datacenters consume megawatts**: Training large AI models requires staggering amounts of computation—trillions of operations per second across thousands of processors. While each operation is efficient relative to what it accomplishes, the sheer scale means enormous energy consumption. The biggest training runs can use as much electricity as a small city.

## The Entropy Connection

Here's the deep principle: **Information is the opposite of entropy**.

Entropy is disorder, spread-out-ness, randomness. When Lincoln's crayons are scattered, the system has high entropy.

When I gather them back into the box, I'm reducing entropy. But I can't do that for free—the universe won't let me. I have to pay with energy (calories burned walking around).

But if I **know where they are**, I can reduce entropy much more efficiently. Knowledge lets me violate the normal rules—locally, temporarily—because I'm using information to guide my actions.

The technical way to say this: **Information is negative entropy**. Knowing something is equivalent to having a lower-entropy description of the world.

## The Answer

So what's the energy of knowledge?

**Acquisition cost**: The real cost—about 0.02 joules to see and remember 20 crayon locations  
**Storage cost**: Microscopic—theoretically as low as 10⁻¹⁹ joules, but biologically about 10⁻¹⁰ joules  
**Maintenance cost**: Tiny—a few microwatts to keep in working memory  
**Value in action**: Macroscopic—28,000 joules saved in this example  
**Deletion cost**: Fixed by physics—kT ln(2) per bit minimum when erasing

When I know where Lincoln's crayons are, that knowledge:
- Cost me 0.02 joules to acquire (seeing them scatter, processing, remembering)
- Saves me 28,000 joules of searching (1.4 million times what I spent)
- Exists as physical patterns in my neurons (negligible storage energy)
- Will cost a tiny amount to forget (as my brain recycles those neurons)

**The key insight**: The return on investment is what matters. Evolution doesn't care about approaching theoretical limits—it cares that investing 0.02 joules in brain activity saves 28,000 joules in muscle activity. That 10⁶-fold return explains why nervous systems exist.

The profound insight: **Knowledge has thermodynamic weight**. It's not separate from the physical world. Information is as real as energy, and the two are deeply connected.

Every time you remember where you put your keys, every time your GPS tells you the fastest route, every time a cell reads its DNA—the universe is doing information-to-energy conversions.

And it's all there in the physics, waiting to be discovered by a curious mind asking: "Dad, why is it hard to put the crayons back?"

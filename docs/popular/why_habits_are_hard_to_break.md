# Why Habits Are Hard to Break

You have tried to quit something. Maybe coffee, maybe checking your phone first thing in the morning, maybe biting your nails. You decided to stop. You meant it. You really meant it.

And then, three days later — or three hours later — you were doing it again.

Everyone has this experience. And the standard explanation is willpower: you did not have enough of it. If you were stronger, more disciplined, more committed, you would have succeeded.

That explanation is wrong. Or at least, it is missing the physics.

<!-- more -->

## What mass actually means

In physics, mass has a very specific meaning. It is not weight. It is not size. Mass is **resistance to acceleration**.

Push a shopping cart. It accelerates easily. Push a loaded truck. It barely moves. The truck has more mass — more resistance to being moved from its current state of motion.

Newton wrote this down:

$$F = \mathcal{M}a$$

Force equals mass times acceleration. Rearrange it: $a = F / \mathcal{M}$. The more mass, the less acceleration you get from the same push.

Now here is the key move. Forget about shopping carts. Think about habits.

A habit is a pattern of behavior that resists change. You push against it — you decide to stop, you set an alarm, you throw away the cigarettes — and it barely budges. Then the push fades, and you snap right back.

That is mass. Not metaphorical mass. Actual, formal, mathematically identical mass — just operating in a different state space.

Instead of position in physical space, the state is your behavior pattern. Instead of physical force, the push is your intention, your environment change, your new routine. And instead of physical mass, the resistance is **generalized mass** — denoted $\mathcal{M}$ — which measures how much a system resists changing its state, regardless of what that state represents.

The equation is the same: $F = \mathcal{M}\ddot{X}$. Force equals generalized mass times the second derivative of the generalized coordinate. Your habit has high $\mathcal{M}$, so it takes enormous force to produce even modest acceleration.

## Why some habits are harder than others

But here is something the everyday willpower story completely misses: habits do not resist equally in all directions.

Think about your morning coffee routine. You might find it easy to change *when* you drink coffee — shifting from 7 AM to 8 AM is no big deal. But changing *whether* you drink coffee at all? That is a completely different magnitude of difficulty.

Same habit. Two different directions of change. Two wildly different resistances.

This is not a quirk. It is a fundamental property of how mass works in complex systems. In physics, mass is not always a single number. For objects that can move in multiple directions, mass is a **tensor** — a matrix that tells you the resistance in each direction and in each combination of directions.

$$\mathcal{M}_{ij} = \text{resistance to change in direction } i \text{ when pushed in direction } j$$

For your coffee habit:
- $\mathcal{M}_{\text{timing, timing}}$ (resistance to changing timing) might be small
- $\mathcal{M}_{\text{whether, whether}}$ (resistance to changing whether) might be enormous
- $\mathcal{M}_{\text{timing, whether}}$ (how timing change couples to whether change) might tell you something useful — maybe gradually shifting your coffee later and later is a path toward dropping it entirely

The mass tensor is why blanket advice like "just quit" is bad physics. It ignores the directional structure of the resistance. Good habit-change strategies — the ones that actually work — are implicitly finding the low-mass directions and using them.

## The stability basin

There is another piece of physics that makes habits sticky: the stability basin.

Imagine a marble sitting at the bottom of a bowl. Push it a little. It rolls up the side, slows down, and rolls back to the bottom. You have to push it hard enough to get it *over the rim* — otherwise it always comes back.

Your habit sits in a basin like this. The basin is a region in your behavioral state space where small perturbations get absorbed. You skip coffee one morning. You feel terrible. By afternoon you are back to coffee. The basin pulled you back.

The depth of the basin determines how much perturbation the habit can absorb without changing. A shallow basin — a habit you picked up last week — can be disrupted by a small push. A deep basin — a habit you have had for twenty years — requires a massive, sustained force to escape.

This is why the "21 days to form a habit" idea is misleading. It is not about time. It is about basin depth. Some habits carve deep basins quickly (anything involving chemical reinforcement, like caffeine or nicotine). Others remain shallow for years (flossing, stretching, journaling). The basin depth depends on the feedback loops that reinforce the pattern, not on the calendar.

## How habits actually break

If a habit has high generalized mass and sits in a deep stability basin, how does anyone ever change?

Three ways, all of which have clear physics:

**1. Overwhelming force.** Apply a push so large it exceeds the basin depth. This is the "cold turkey" approach. It works when you can sustain enormous force long enough to clear the rim. It fails when the force fades before you escape the basin — and now you have spent energy and gone nowhere.

**2. Reshaping the basin.** Change the environment so the basin becomes shallower. Remove the coffee maker. Move to a place where your trigger does not exist. Change your schedule so the habit's slot disappears. You are not fighting the mass directly — you are changing the landscape so the same mass no longer holds you in place.

**3. Finding the low-mass direction.** Use the tensor structure. Instead of attacking the habit head-on (high $\mathcal{M}$), find a direction where resistance is low and push there. Switch from coffee to tea. Change the *kind* of phone-checking you do. Substitute rather than eliminate. The habit's mass in the "substitute" direction may be far lower than in the "quit entirely" direction.

Most successful habit-change strategies use some combination of all three — and they work precisely because they are respecting the physics, even when the person using them has never heard of a mass tensor.

## The physics you already know

You already know everything in this post. You know that old habits are harder to break than new ones (deeper basin). You know that some changes are easier than others (mass tensor). You know that changing your environment matters (reshaping the basin). You know that willpower alone often is not enough (insufficient force for the mass involved).

What you did not have was the equation. And the equation matters — not because you need to compute anything, but because it tells you that these are not separate observations. They are all consequences of a single structure: generalized mass operating in behavioral state space.

The same $F = \mathcal{M}\ddot{X}$ that describes a truck resisting a push describes your habit resisting your intention. The same mass tensor that describes an object easier to slide than to lift describes your coffee habit easier to reschedule than to quit. The same stability basin that keeps a marble in a bowl keeps you in your routine.

One framework. Every domain.
